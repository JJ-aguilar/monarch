Classes throughout the Arlington Career Center have worked together to plant a garden that will sustain pollinators, with a focus on the monarch butterfly. The monarch butterfly makes an iconic 2,500 mile round-trip journey between Canada and Mexico every year. Due to factors like pollution, pesticides, and habitat loss, this migration is in danger of ending. Students at the Career Center have held two fundraisers to procure money for the materials needed to plant a monarch garden, and to purchase native plants which will sustain both the caterpillars and the butterflies along their migration path. Additionally, students have added native plants to an existing garden outside the main doors of the Career Center.

Butterfly Migration

We are working to support the butterflies as they migrate between Canada and the biosphere in central Mexico. ACC students created a garden to sustain the monarch butterflies with nectar plants and to provide milkweeds, which are the one plants the butterfly caterpillars can eat. We have a sister school in Mexico. Students there are planting oyamel trees, which are the trees the monarch overwinter on.

Our goals are:

  • Learning about monarchs by studying them and raising them
  • Communicating with students in Mexico
  • Maintaining the garden to sustain the species
  • Educating the community to plant native plants to help the butterflies
