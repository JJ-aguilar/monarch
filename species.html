Species

Monarchs belong to the class of animals known as insects. Monarchs belong to the Order Lepidoptera, which means "scaly wings." This order of animals incudes butterflies and moths.

Most insects eat different food as adults from the food they ate as juveniles. The reason native plants are so intrinsic to the health of many pollinators is that many have evolved with specific plants, and require those plants for survival. Monarch butterflies (and many lepidoptera) feed on flowers as adults, using their long proboscises to suck nectar from the plants. Monarch caterpillars, on the other hand,are "specialists." They can only eat and develop from the leaves of milkweed plants. A significant drop in the milkweed population, therefore, equates to a significant drop in the monarch population.

Why these creatures are important

All living creatures living in temperate climates must have a strategy to survive the winter months. Many insects survive in the warm hollows of leaf litter or tree bark. Others overwinter in the soil. Still others overwinter as pupa attached to a tree branch or other surface. Eastern North American monarchs have developed another strategy: They undertake a 2,000 mile migration between Canada and Mexico twice a year.

Their destination is an oyamel fir forest in Michoacan Province, Mexico, where they roost in dense colonies in the trees. The oyamel fir forest is critical to the monarchsâ€™ survival. It creates a microclimate with the perfect overwintering conditions: moist mountain habitat which remains cool without freezing. Monarchs cling together in clumps on the trees and leave occasionally on warm days to get water from nearby sources. Monarchs begin arriving at their overwintering site in early November, often coinciding with local celebrations of the Day of the Dead on November 1st. Local people have long associated the monarchs with the souls of their departed loved ones, and the first arrivals are met with great celebration!
