World Issues

Over the past 50 years, scientists have been recording steady drops in the monarch populations, particularly with the Eastern North American population which completes the miraculous 2,000 mile annual migration previously described. Many factors contribute to the drop in numbers. We will briefly describe the most critical factors:

Habitat Loss in In Mexico:

In Mexico, drastic habitat losses have occurred on many levels. Since Dr. Fred Urquhart first deciphered the monarch migration to Mexico in the early 1970’s (prior to his research no one knew where the monarchs had gone), an alarming reduction of the oyamel forest sanctuary has occurred, primarily due to logging. This protected natural area of the monarch butterfly was established in 1980 by the Mexican government to protect the overwintering sites. Since the decree was largely ineffective, the government established the Monarch Butterfly Special Biosphere Reserve in 1986. The new decree protected land used by 5 monarch colonies, a total area of 16,110 hectares, or acres.

The government prohibits logging in the “core” zone of the Biosphere and regulates logging in a buffer zone. Since the legislation did not factor in the natural movement of colonies in February and March (as they prepare to migrate north), the government issues a new decree in 2000 to expand the reserve to 42,707 hectares. Logging was still allowed in the buffer zones.

The problem of habitat loss in Mexico, however, is far from resolved. Much of the land in the Biosphere is loosely owned by local families, who own farming rights to many parcels. The local people frequently clear trees for farming purposes, as well as for building and firewood.

Habitat Loss in North America:

North America is experiencing drastic losses in milkweed populations. Milkweed often grows naturally along roadsides and in open fields. Milkweeds contain glycosides, toxins that can cause sickness in animals, including people.* Farmers often clear grazing areas to protect their livestock. The sprays they use on the milkweeds eliminate not only the plants, but also kill many pollinators that feed from the flowers and plants. Additionally, development of land, as well as our human obsession with green lawns, has destroyed a great deal of milkweed habitat.

Climate Change

Climate change poses a long-term threat to the monarch migration. A model projecting conditions in the Biosphere in 2050 show that conditions in the forest which monarchs require for their survival will be drastically altered. Increases in rainfall and freezing temperatures will render the forest unusable to monarchs. The ongoing logging operations have so reduced the buffer zone around the Biosphere that the butterflies have little protection from severe weather events. In recent years, Mexico has experienced several severe weather events. Each time, these events have drastically reduced the populations of overwintering monarch.
